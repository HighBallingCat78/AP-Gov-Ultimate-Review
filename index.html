01
Basic Economic Concepts
~5–10% of exam · Big ideas: Scarcity, Trade-offs, Opportunity Cost, Comparative Advantage
UNIT 1
🔑 Scarcity & Trade-offs
  • Scarcity: Unlimited wants vs. limited resources — the fundamental economic problem
  • Opportunity cost: The next-best alternative foregone when a choice is made
  • Trade-off: Every economic choice involves giving something up
  • Factors of production: Land, Labor, Capital, Entrepreneurship
  • Ceteris paribus: "All else equal" — holds other variables constant in analysis
📈 Production Possibilities Curve (PPC)
  • PPC: Shows maximum combinations of two goods an economy can produce given current resources
  • Points ON the curve: Efficient (fully employed resources)
  • Points INSIDE the curve: Inefficient (unemployment or underutilization)
  • Points OUTSIDE the curve: Currently unattainable
  • Outward shift: Economic growth — more resources, better technology
  • Concave (bowed out) shape: Law of increasing opportunity cost
🌐 Comparative & Absolute Advantage
  • Absolute advantage: Producing more output with same inputs than another
  • Comparative advantage: Producing at a lower opportunity cost — basis for trade
  • Specialization: Produce the good in which you have comparative advantage
  • Gains from trade: Both parties benefit by specializing and trading
  • Terms of trade: Must fall between each country's opportunity costs for mutual benefit
🏛 Economic Systems & Circular Flow
  • Command economy: Government makes all production decisions
  • Market economy: Prices and markets coordinate decisions
  • Mixed economy: Combination of market and government intervention
  • Circular flow: Households supply resources to firms; firms pay wages/rent/interest/profit
  • Product markets: Goods and services exchanged between firms and households
  • Factor markets: Resources (land, labor, capital) exchanged
02
Economic Indicators & Business Cycle
~12–17% of exam · Big ideas: GDP, Unemployment, Inflation, Business Cycle
UNIT 2
📊 Gross Domestic Product (GDP)
  • GDP: Total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country in a year
  • Expenditure approach: GDP = C + I + G + (X − M)
  • C: Consumption (households) — largest component (~70% in the U.S.)
  • I: Investment (business spending, residential construction)
  • G: Government spending (not transfer payments)
  • X − M: Net exports (exports minus imports)
  • Nominal GDP: Measured in current prices — affected by inflation
  • Real GDP: Adjusted for price level changes using base year prices
  • GDP Deflator: (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) × 100
👷 Unemployment
  • Unemployment rate: (Unemployed / Labor Force) × 100
  • Labor force: Employed + actively looking for work (excludes discouraged workers)
  • Frictional unemployment: Between jobs; short-term, natural
  • Structural unemployment: Skills mismatch; displaced by technology or industry shifts
  • Cyclical unemployment: Due to recession; decreases in expansions
  • Seasonal unemployment: Predictable changes due to season
  • Natural Rate of Unemployment (NRU): Frictional + structural; ~4–5%
  • Full employment: Only NRU exists — cyclical = 0
  • Discouraged workers: Stopped looking; NOT counted as unemployed
💹 Inflation & Price Indices
  • Inflation: Sustained increase in the overall price level
  • Deflation: Sustained decrease in the price level
  • CPI (Consumer Price Index): Measures price changes of a fixed market basket of consumer goods
  • Inflation rate: % change in CPI = [(CPI₂ − CPI₁) / CPI₁] × 100
  • Demand-pull inflation: "Too much money chasing too few goods" — AD shifts right
  • Cost-push inflation: Supply shock raises costs (AS shifts left); stagflation
  • Real vs. nominal: Real = Nominal − Inflation rate (Fisher equation)
  • Costs of inflation: Hurts savers, creditors, fixed-income earners; helps debtors
🔄 Business Cycle
  • Expansion: Rising real GDP, falling unemployment, rising prices
  • Peak: Maximum output; low unemployment; potential inflation pressures
  • Contraction/Recession: Falling real GDP for 2+ consecutive quarters; rising unemployment
  • Trough: Lowest point; high unemployment, low inflation
  • Leading indicators: Stock prices, building permits, consumer confidence — predict future
  • Lagging indicators: Unemployment rate, CPI — confirm what already happened
  • Output gap: Actual GDP minus Potential GDP; negative = recessionary gap
⚠️ GDP Exclusions — What Does NOT Count

Intermediate goods (flour used in bread), used goods (resold car), non-market activities (home cooking), financial transactions (stock purchases), transfer payments (Social Security, unemployment benefits), illegal activities.

03
National Income & Price Determination
~17–27% of exam · Big ideas: AD-AS Model, Fiscal Policy, Multipliers
UNIT 3
📉 Aggregate Demand (AD)
  • AD: Total spending in the economy at various price levels
  • AD = C + I + G + (X − M)
  • AD slopes downward because of wealth, interest rate, and foreign purchases effects
  • Shifters of AD (increase → right shift):
  • ↑ Consumer confidence, ↑ wealth, ↑ government spending, ↑ exports, ↓ taxes, ↓ interest rates
  • Decrease → left shift: ↑ taxes, ↓ gov't spending, ↑ interest rates, ↓ consumer confidence
📈 Aggregate Supply (AS)
  • Short-Run AS (SRAS): Upward-sloping; input prices are fixed in short run
  • Long-Run AS (LRAS): Vertical at potential/full-employment GDP (Q*)
  • SRAS shifts RIGHT (increase): ↓ Input costs (wages, oil), ↑ productivity, ↑ subsidies
  • SRAS shifts LEFT (decrease): ↑ Input costs, supply shocks (oil price spike)
  • LRAS shifts RIGHT: More resources, better technology, improved institutions
⚖️ AD-AS Equilibrium & Gaps
  • Equilibrium: Where AD intersects SRAS — determines real GDP and price level
  • Recessionary gap: Actual GDP < Potential GDP; high unemployment
  • Inflationary gap: Actual GDP > Potential GDP; upward wage/price pressure
  • Long-run self-correction: Wages adjust; SRAS shifts until reaching LRAS (slow!)
  • Stagflation: Negative supply shock — SRAS shifts left → ↑ prices + ↑ unemployment simultaneously
🏛 Fiscal Policy
  • Fiscal policy: Government use of spending and taxation to stabilize the economy
  • Expansionary fiscal policy: ↑ Gov't spending or ↓ taxes → shifts AD right → closes recessionary gap
  • Contractionary fiscal policy: ↓ Gov't spending or ↑ taxes → shifts AD left → closes inflationary gap
  • Discretionary fiscal policy: Deliberate legislative actions
  • Automatic stabilizers: Unemployment insurance, progressive taxes — automatically stabilize without new legislation
  • Crowding out: Gov't borrowing raises interest rates → reduces private investment
✖️ Multipliers
  • Spending multiplier: 1 / (1 − MPC) = 1 / MPS
  • Tax multiplier: −MPC / MPS (negative — tax cut raises income less than direct spending)
  • Balanced budget multiplier: Equal ↑ spending and ↑ taxes → multiplier = 1
  • MPC (Marginal Propensity to Consume): Fraction of each additional dollar spent
  • MPS (Marginal Propensity to Save): Fraction of each additional dollar saved; MPC + MPS = 1
  • Example: MPC = 0.8 → Spending multiplier = 1/0.2 = 5
📊 Phillips Curve
  • Short-run Phillips Curve (SRPC): Inverse relationship between inflation and unemployment
  • Long-run Phillips Curve (LRPC): Vertical at the Natural Rate of Unemployment — no trade-off in long run
  • Expansionary policy: Moves left along SRPC (↓ unemployment, ↑ inflation)
  • Contractionary policy: Moves right along SRPC (↑ unemployment, ↓ inflation)
  • Supply shock shifts SRPC: Cost-push inflation → SRPC shifts right (stagflation)
  • LRPC location: Determined by NRU; can shift with structural changes
04
Financial Sector
~18–23% of exam · Big ideas: Money, Banking, Monetary Policy, Loanable Funds
UNIT 4
💵 Money & Its Functions
  • Functions of money: Medium of exchange, unit of account, store of value
  • M1 (narrow money): Currency in circulation + demand deposits (checking accounts)
  • M2 (broad money): M1 + savings deposits + small time deposits + money market funds
  • Commodity money: Has intrinsic value (gold coins)
  • Fiat money: Value by government decree — no intrinsic value (paper currency)
  • Liquidity: How easily an asset converts to cash; M1 most liquid
🏦 Banking & Money Creation
  • Fractional reserve banking: Banks hold only a fraction of deposits as reserves
  • Reserve requirement (RR): % of deposits banks must hold; set by the Fed
  • Excess reserves: Actual reserves − Required reserves; can be loaned out
  • Money multiplier: 1 / Reserve Requirement
  • Money creation example: $1,000 deposit, 10% RR → up to $10,000 created in economy
  • T-account: Balance sheet showing assets (loans + reserves) and liabilities (deposits)
🏛 The Federal Reserve (The Fed)
  • Federal Reserve: U.S. central bank; conducts monetary policy
  • Dual mandate: Maximum employment + stable prices (2% inflation target)
  • Federal funds rate: Interest rate banks charge each other for overnight loans — the Fed's primary target
  • Open market operations (OMO): Buying/selling Treasury bonds — primary tool
  • Buy bonds → ↑ money supply → ↓ interest rates (expansionary)
  • Sell bonds → ↓ money supply → ↑ interest rates (contractionary)
  • Discount rate: Rate the Fed charges banks for loans; ↓ = expansionary
  • Reserve requirement: ↓ RR = expansionary; rarely changed
  • Interest on reserves (IOR): Fed pays banks to hold excess reserves
📊 Money Market Graph
  • Vertical supply (Ms): Fed controls money supply — not interest-rate sensitive
  • Downward-sloping demand (Md): Higher interest rates → less money demanded for transactions
  • Equilibrium: Nominal interest rate where Ms = Md
  • Fed buys bonds → Ms shifts right → nominal interest rate falls
  • Fed sells bonds → Ms shifts left → nominal interest rate rises
  • ↑ GDP or ↑ price level → Md shifts right → ↑ interest rate
💰 Loanable Funds Market
  • Loanable funds market: Where savers (supply) meet borrowers (demand); price = real interest rate
  • Supply of loanable funds: Savings — upward sloping; ↑ real interest rate → ↑ saving
  • Demand for loanable funds: Investment — downward sloping; ↑ real interest rate → ↓ investment
  • Shifts in supply: ↑ savings rate, ↑ foreign capital inflows, gov't surplus → right shift
  • Shifts in demand: ↑ investment opportunities, government deficit spending → right shift
  • Fisher effect: Real rate ≈ Nominal rate − Inflation rate
🔁 Monetary Policy Transmission Mechanism

Fed buys bonds → ↑ bank reserves → ↑ money supply → ↓ nominal interest rates → ↑ investment (I) and consumption (C) → AD shifts right → ↑ real GDP and ↑ price level. Works in reverse for contractionary policy.

05
Long-Run Consequences of Stabilization Policies
~20–30% of exam · Big ideas: Long-Run Growth, Deficits, Debt, Inflation Expectations
UNIT 5
📈 Economic Growth & LRAS
  • Long-run economic growth: Rightward shift of LRAS (= rightward shift of PPC)
  • Sources of growth: More capital, better technology, improved human capital, more labor
  • Investment in capital: Key to shifting LRAS right — requires saving today
  • Rule of 70: Years to double GDP ≈ 70 / growth rate
  • Productivity: Output per unit of input — the engine of long-run growth
💸 Deficits, Debt & Crowding Out
  • Budget deficit: Government spending > Tax revenue in a given year
  • National debt: Cumulative sum of all past budget deficits
  • Crowding out: Gov't borrowing increases demand for loanable funds → ↑ real interest rate → ↓ private investment
  • Complete crowding out: Every dollar of gov't spending displaces one dollar of private investment
  • Partial crowding out: Gov't borrowing raises rates somewhat but investment doesn't fall by full amount
🔄 Inflation Expectations & Long-Run Adjustments
  • Adaptive expectations: People base future inflation expectations on past inflation
  • Rational expectations: People use all available info, including policy announcements
  • Wage-price spiral: ↑ inflation → workers demand higher wages → firms raise prices → more inflation
  • Long-run self-correction: If AD ↑ beyond potential, wages eventually rise, SRAS shifts left, returning to LRAS
  • Hyperinflation: Extremely rapid inflation; destroys purchasing power; caused by excessive money creation
⚡ Supply-Side Policies
  • Supply-side economics: Policies aimed at shifting LRAS (and SRAS) to the right
  • Tax cuts on investment: ↑ after-tax return → ↑ capital investment → ↑ productivity
  • Deregulation: ↓ business costs → ↑ output
  • Education & training: ↑ human capital → ↑ productivity → LRAS shifts right
  • Infrastructure investment: Improves productivity across the economy
  • Laffer Curve: Tax rate vs. tax revenue — very high rates may reduce revenue by discouraging work
06
Open Economy: International Trade & Finance
~10–15% of exam · Big ideas: Balance of Payments, Exchange Rates, Current & Capital Accounts
UNIT 6
📋 Balance of Payments
  • Balance of payments: Record of all economic transactions between a country and the world
  • Current account: Trade in goods (merchandise trade) + services + income + transfers
  • Trade deficit: Imports > Exports; negative current account balance
  • Trade surplus: Exports > Imports; positive current account balance
  • Capital/Financial account: Foreign investment flows in and out of a country
  • Balance of payments must balance: Current account deficit = Capital account surplus
💱 Exchange Rates
  • Exchange rate: Price of one currency in terms of another
  • Appreciation: Currency gains value relative to another (buys more foreign currency)
  • Depreciation: Currency loses value (buys less foreign currency)
  • ↑ Demand for USD → USD appreciates
  • Flexible (floating) exchange rate: Determined by supply and demand in currency markets
  • Fixed exchange rate: Government maintains a set value; requires intervention
🔗 Exchange Rate Effects on Trade
  • USD appreciates → U.S. exports more expensive → ↓ exports, ↑ imports → trade deficit widens
  • USD depreciates → U.S. exports cheaper → ↑ exports, ↓ imports → trade deficit narrows
  • Interest rate link: ↑ U.S. interest rates → ↑ demand for USD → USD appreciates
  • Monetary policy connection: Expansionary policy → ↓ interest rates → ↓ demand for USD → USD depreciates → ↑ net exports → AD shifts further right
🌍 Foreign Exchange Market (FOREX)
  • Demand for USD: Foreigners want to buy U.S. goods, services, or assets
  • Supply of USD: Americans want to buy foreign goods, services, or assets
  • Equilibrium: Exchange rate where quantity demanded = quantity supplied
  • Right shift in demand for USD: ↑ U.S. interest rates, ↑ U.S. growth, ↑ foreign demand for U.S. goods
  • Right shift in supply of USD: ↑ U.S. demand for imports, ↓ U.S. interest rates
🔁 The Full Policy Linkage Chain (Open Economy)

Expansionary monetary policy → ↓ U.S. interest rates → ↓ demand for USD (capital flows out) → USD depreciates → U.S. exports become cheaper abroad, imports more expensive → ↑ net exports (X−M) → AD shifts right further. This amplifies the effect of monetary policy in an open economy.

① Production Possibilities Curve (PPC)
Unit 1 · Shows the maximum combinations of two goods an economy can produce · Bowed-out shape = law of increasing opportunity cost
Baseline PPC — Points & Efficiency
Points on curve = efficient. Inside = inefficient. Outside = unattainable.

Key Points

  • On curve: Fully efficient — all resources used
  • Inside curve: Recession or unemployment
  • Outside curve: Not currently possible
  • Slope: Opportunity cost of Good B in terms of Good A
Outward Shift → Economic Growth
New PPC₁ lies outside original PPC₀. More of both goods now attainable.

Causes of Outward Shift

  • ↑ Resources: More labor, capital, land
  • ↑ Technology: Better production methods
  • ↑ Human capital: Better education/training
  • ↑ Investment: More capital accumulation
One-Sided Shift → Tech in One Sector
Technology improves only for Good B → curve rotates outward on B-axis only.

Key Insight

  • Max Good A unchanged (y-intercept stays)
  • Max Good B increases (x-intercept moves right)
  • Curve pivots, not parallel shift
  • Example: Technology improves only in agriculture
② AD-AS Model — The Most Tested Graph
Unit 3 · Axes: Price Level (y) vs. Real GDP (x) · Three curves: AD, SRAS, LRAS · Most FRQ points come from here
Baseline — Full Employment
AD intersects SRAS exactly at LRAS. Economy at potential GDP (Q*).

This Is Your Starting Point

  • All three curves intersect at one point
  • No gap exists — economy is healthy
  • Always draw this first, then apply the shock
Recessionary Gap — AD Shifts Left
↓ Consumer confidence, ↑ taxes, ↓ gov't spending → AD shifts left → output falls below Q*.

AD Shifts LEFT when:

  • ↓ Consumer confidence/wealth
  • ↑ Taxes (less disposable income)
  • ↓ Government spending
  • ↑ Interest rates (Fed sells bonds)
  • ↓ Net exports (↑ exchange rate)
Inflationary Gap — AD Shifts Right
↑ Gov't spending, ↓ taxes, ↑ consumer confidence → AD shifts right → output exceeds Q*.

AD Shifts RIGHT when:

  • ↑ Gov't spending (fiscal stimulus)
  • ↓ Taxes → ↑ disposable income
  • ↑ Consumer/business confidence
  • ↓ Interest rates (Fed buys bonds)
  • ↑ Net exports (↓ exchange rate)
Negative Supply Shock — Stagflation
Oil price spike, natural disaster → SRAS shifts LEFT → ↑ prices + ↓ GDP simultaneously.

SRAS Shifts LEFT when:

  • ↑ Input costs: Oil, wages, raw materials
  • Supply shock: Natural disasters, crop failures
  • Result: STAGFLATION — the worst of both worlds
  • No easy fix: Expansionary cures ↓Q but worsens ↑P
LRAS Shift Right — Long-Run Growth
Better tech, more capital, improved institutions → LRAS shifts right → potential GDP increases.

LRAS Shifts RIGHT (Growth) from:

  • ↑ Technology: R&D, innovation
  • ↑ Physical capital: Investment, infrastructure
  • ↑ Human capital: Education, training
  • ↑ Labor force: Population growth, immigration
  • Better institutions: Property rights, rule of law
③ Money Market
Unit 4 · Axes: Nominal Interest Rate (y) vs. Quantity of Money (x) · Ms is VERTICAL — Fed controls it · Md slopes down
Fed Buys Bonds → Ms Shifts Right → ↓ Interest Rate
Expansionary monetary policy. Money supply increases → nominal interest rate falls.

Expansionary Monetary Policy

  • Fed buys Treasury bonds
  • ↑ Bank reserves → ↑ Ms
  • ↓ Nominal interest rate
  • → ↑ Investment → AD shifts right
  • Also: ↓ Discount rate, ↓ Reserve requirement
Fed Sells Bonds → Ms Shifts Left → ↑ Interest Rate
Contractionary monetary policy. Money supply decreases → nominal interest rate rises.

Contractionary Monetary Policy

  • Fed sells Treasury bonds
  • ↓ Bank reserves → ↓ Ms
  • ↑ Nominal interest rate
  • → ↓ Investment → AD shifts left
  • Also: ↑ Discount rate, ↑ Reserve requirement
↑ GDP or ↑ Price Level → Md Shifts Right
Higher income or prices → more transactions needed → demand for money rises → interest rate rises.

Md Shifts RIGHT when:

  • ↑ Real GDP (more transactions)
  • ↑ Price level (same goods cost more)
  • Both cause: ↑ nominal interest rate
  • Ms unchanged — Fed didn't act
④ Loanable Funds Market
Unit 4 · Axes: Real Interest Rate (y) vs. Quantity of Loanable Funds (x) · Supply = Saving · Demand = Investment borrowing
↑ Gov't Deficit → D Shifts Right → Crowding Out
Government borrows more → competes with private borrowers → real interest rate rises → private investment falls.

Demand Shifts RIGHT when:

  • ↑ Gov't deficit → gov't borrows more
  • ↑ Business investment demand
  • Result: ↑ real interest rate → ↓ private investment
  • Crowding out = fiscal policy partially offset
↑ Savings → S Shifts Right → ↓ Real Interest Rate
More saving → more funds available → real rate falls → private investment rises.

Supply Shifts RIGHT when:

  • ↑ Household saving rate
  • ↑ Foreign capital inflows
  • Gov't budget surplus
  • Result: ↓ real interest rate → ↑ investment
⑤ Phillips Curve (SRPC & LRPC)
Unit 3 · Axes: Inflation Rate (y) vs. Unemployment Rate (x) · SRPC slopes down · LRPC is vertical at NRU · MIRRORS the AD-AS model
Baseline SRPC + LRPC
Short-run trade-off: move along SRPC. Long-run: always return to NRU on LRPC.

The Big Picture

  • SRPC: Trade-off in the short run only
  • LRPC: Vertical — no trade-off long run
  • Long run: Economy always returns to NRU
  • NRU ≈ 4–5% in U.S.
Expansionary Policy → Move Up-Left Along SRPC
Gov't stimulus or Fed buys bonds → ↑ AD → ↓ unemployment, ↑ inflation. Move up and left along curve.

Movement Along SRPC

  • Expansionary: move up-left (↓U, ↑π)
  • Contractionary: move down-right (↑U, ↓π)
  • This is a movement along, not a shift
  • Short run only — long run returns to LRPC
Supply Shock → SRPC Shifts RIGHT (Stagflation)
Oil price spike → SRAS shifts left → SRPC shifts RIGHT → both inflation and unemployment rise simultaneously.

SRPC Shifts RIGHT when:

  • Negative supply shock (oil, disasters)
  • ↑ Inflation expectations
  • Result: ↑ unemployment AND ↑ inflation
  • This is STAGFLATION — no easy cure
  • SRPC shifts LEFT with positive supply shocks
⑥ Foreign Exchange Market (FOREX)
Unit 6 · Axes: Exchange Rate — value of USD (y) vs. Quantity of USD (x) · D slopes down · S slopes up
↑ U.S. Interest Rates → Demand for USD ↑ → Appreciates
Higher U.S. rates → foreigners want U.S. assets → buy USD → demand shifts right → USD appreciates.

Demand for USD Shifts RIGHT when:

  • ↑ U.S. interest rates
  • ↑ U.S. economic growth
  • ↑ Foreign demand for U.S. goods
  • Result: USD appreciates → ↓ exports, ↑ imports
Expansionary Monetary Policy → USD Depreciates
Fed buys bonds → ↓ interest rates → less attractive to foreigners → demand for USD falls → depreciates.

Demand for USD Shifts LEFT when:

  • ↓ U.S. interest rates (expansionary MP)
  • ↑ U.S. inflation (goods less competitive)
  • Result: USD depreciates → ↑ exports, ↓ imports
  • → ↑ Net exports → AD shifts further right

FRQ Mastery Guide
Section II: 1 Long FRQ (10 pts) + 2 Short FRQs (5 pts each) = 33% of score · 60 minutes
FRQ
01
Long Free-Response Question
10 POINTS · ~25 minutes · multi-part with graphs
  • 1Read the entire question before writing — identify which markets are involved (AD-AS? Money market? Loanable funds? FOREX?)
  • 2Draw each required graph carefully: label axes, curves, equilibrium points (P*, Q*), and direction of all shifts with arrows
  • 3Show original equilibrium AND new equilibrium — use subscripts: P₁, Q₁ for new; never erase original curves
  • 4For "explain" questions: state the mechanism step-by-step (e.g., "The Fed buys bonds → money supply increases → interest rates fall → investment increases → AD shifts right")
  • 5Connect multiple markets: a change in AD-AS often leads to a change in the money market, which affects loanable funds, which affects FOREX — trace the chain
  • 6Identify gaps explicitly: state "this creates a recessionary gap because actual GDP is below potential GDP"
  • 7Answer every sub-part; partial credit is available — an incomplete graph can still earn points for the parts that are correct
02
Short Free-Response Question #1
5 POINTS · ~15 minutes · focused on 1–2 concepts
  • 1Identify the specific topic: fiscal policy? monetary policy? trade? Often focused on one unit
  • 2Be concise and precise — use correct economic terminology; "decrease" not "go down"
  • 3If a graph is asked for, draw it even if the question just says "show" — a labeled graph can earn full points without prose
  • 4State direction of change AND why: "Real GDP increases because the spending multiplier amplifies the initial increase in government spending"
  • 5Avoid vague answers — "the economy improves" earns no credit; "real GDP rises and unemployment falls" does
03
Short Free-Response Question #2
5 POINTS · ~15 minutes · often open economy or financial sector
  • 1Unit 6 (open economy) frequently appears here — be ready to draw and shift the FOREX graph
  • 2Trace interest rate changes to exchange rate changes: "↑ interest rates → ↑ demand for domestic currency → currency appreciates → exports ↓, imports ↑"
  • 3For trade balance questions: remember CA deficit = KA surplus; they must offset
  • 4Use the complete chain for full credit — don't skip steps even if they seem obvious
  • 5Check your answer: does it make economic sense? Would a real central bank make this decision?
🎯 Sample Long FRQ Prompt & Model Outline

Prompt: "Suppose the U.S. economy is operating at full employment. The federal government decides to increase spending on infrastructure without raising taxes. (a) Using a correctly labeled AD-AS graph, show the short-run effect. (b) Identify the gap created. (c) Using a correctly labeled loanable funds graph, show the effect on real interest rates. (d) Explain how the change in interest rates affects private investment. (e) Using a correctly labeled FOREX graph for USD, show the effect on the exchange rate."

Model outline: (a) AD shifts right → PL↑, RGDP↑ above Q* (b) Inflationary gap (c) Gov't borrowing → Demand for LF shifts right → real interest rate rises (d) Higher real interest rate → ↑ cost of borrowing → ↓ private investment (crowding out) (e) ↑ real interest rate → ↑ demand for USD → USD appreciates on FOREX graph.

Example MCQ with Explanations
Section I: 70 questions · 70 minutes · 66% of score
MCQ
QUESTION 1 · UNIT 2 · GDP
Which of the following would be included in the calculation of U.S. GDP?
  • (A) A used car sold at a dealership for $15,000
  • (B) ✓ A new car manufactured in Ohio and sold to a consumer for $35,000
  • (C) Social Security payments received by a retiree
  • (D) Stock purchased on the New York Stock Exchange
Explanation: GDP counts only new, final goods produced domestically. (A) is a used good — already counted. (C) is a transfer payment — no new production. (D) is a financial transaction. (B) is a new final good produced in the U.S. — textbook inclusion.
QUESTION 2 · UNIT 4 · MONETARY POLICY
If the Federal Reserve conducts open market purchases of government bonds, which of the following best describes the immediate effect?
  • (A) The money supply decreases and interest rates rise
  • (B) The money supply decreases and interest rates fall
  • (C) ✓ The money supply increases and interest rates fall
  • (D) The money supply increases and interest rates rise
Explanation: When the Fed buys bonds, it injects reserves into the banking system → money supply shifts right → nominal interest rates fall. This is the core open market operations mechanism — the most tested concept in Unit 4.
QUESTION 3 · UNIT 3 · MULTIPLIER
If the marginal propensity to consume (MPC) is 0.75, what is the spending multiplier?
  • (A) 0.25
  • (B) 1.33
  • (C) 3
  • (D) ✓ 4
Explanation: Spending multiplier = 1 / MPS = 1 / (1 − MPC) = 1 / (1 − 0.75) = 1 / 0.25 = 4. A $100 billion increase in government spending → $400 billion increase in real GDP.
QUESTION 4 · UNIT 2 · UNEMPLOYMENT
An economy is at full employment when which type(s) of unemployment exist?
  • (A) Only frictional unemployment
  • (B) Only structural unemployment
  • (C) ✓ Both frictional and structural unemployment, but no cyclical unemployment
  • (D) No unemployment of any kind
Explanation: Full employment = Natural Rate of Unemployment (NRU) = frictional + structural. Cyclical unemployment = 0 at full employment. Zero unemployment is impossible — workers are always in transition between jobs.
QUESTION 5 · UNIT 6 · EXCHANGE RATES
If U.S. interest rates rise relative to foreign interest rates, what happens to the value of the U.S. dollar and U.S. exports?
  • (A) Dollar depreciates; exports increase
  • (B) ✓ Dollar appreciates; exports decrease
  • (C) Dollar appreciates; exports increase
  • (D) Dollar depreciates; exports decrease
Explanation: ↑ U.S. interest rates → foreign investors want USD assets → ↑ demand for USD → USD appreciates. An appreciated dollar makes U.S. goods more expensive for foreigners → exports decrease. This is the key open economy interest rate–exchange rate–trade chain.
QUESTION 6 · UNIT 3 · AD-AS
A negative supply shock, such as a sudden large increase in oil prices, would most likely result in which of the following?
  • (A) SRAS shifting right, causing real GDP to rise and price level to fall
  • (B) AD shifting left, causing both real GDP and price level to fall
  • (C) ✓ SRAS shifting left, causing real GDP to fall and price level to rise (stagflation)
  • (D) LRAS shifting left, permanently reducing potential output
Explanation: Higher oil prices increase production costs → SRAS shifts left → real GDP falls (recession) AND price level rises (inflation) simultaneously = stagflation. Neither fiscal nor monetary policy can cure both problems at once.
QUESTION 7 · UNIT 4 · BANKING
If a bank receives a $500 deposit and the reserve requirement is 20%, what is the maximum amount the bank can loan out from this deposit?
  • (A) $500
  • (B) $100
  • (C) ✓ $400
  • (D) $2,500
Explanation: Required reserves = $500 × 0.20 = $100. Excess reserves = $500 − $100 = $400, which the bank can loan. The entire banking system could create up to $500 × (1/0.20) = $2,500 total — but one bank can only loan its excess reserves.
QUESTION 8 · UNIT 3 · FISCAL POLICY
Which of the following is an example of an automatic stabilizer?
  • (A) Congress passes a new stimulus package during a recession
  • (B) The Federal Reserve lowers the federal funds rate
  • (C) ✓ Unemployment insurance payments increase as the economy enters a recession
  • (D) The government raises the minimum wage
Explanation: Automatic stabilizers require no new legislation — they kick in automatically. Unemployment insurance spending rises automatically when unemployment rises, helping prop up AD without Congress acting. (A) is discretionary fiscal policy.
QUESTION 9 · UNIT 5 · CROWDING OUT
Which of the following best explains the crowding-out effect of expansionary fiscal policy?
  • (A) Government spending directly replaces private consumption
  • (B) Higher taxes reduce consumer spending
  • (C) ✓ Government borrowing raises real interest rates, reducing private investment
  • (D) Higher inflation caused by fiscal policy discourages saving
Explanation: Gov't borrows in the loanable funds market → demand for loanable funds shifts right → real interest rate rises → private investment falls. This partially or fully offsets the expansionary effect of the fiscal policy.
QUESTION 10 · UNIT 1 · COMPARATIVE ADVANTAGE
Country A can produce 10 shirts or 5 computers per hour. Country B can produce 6 shirts or 4 computers per hour. Which country has the comparative advantage in shirts?
  • (A) ✓ Country A, because the opportunity cost of one shirt is 0.5 computers vs. 0.67 computers for Country B
  • (B) Country B, because it has a lower absolute output
  • (C) Both countries have equal comparative advantage in shirts
  • (D) Country A, because it can produce more shirts in absolute terms
Explanation: Country A: 1 shirt costs 5/10 = 0.5 computers. Country B: 1 shirt costs 4/6 = 0.67 computers. Country A has the lower opportunity cost for shirts → comparative advantage in shirts. Country B has comparative advantage in computers.

Exam Tips, Tricks & Strategy
Tested strategies for the MCQ, FRQ, graphs, and mental game
TIPS
⏱️
MCQ Time Management
70 questions in 70 minutes = 1 minute per question. Skip and flag anything taking more than 90 seconds. Return to flagged questions after finishing. Never leave a bubble blank — no penalty for guessing on AP exams.
📊
Label Everything on Graphs
Every unlabeled axis, curve, or equilibrium point is a lost FRQ point. Before drawing, ask: Do I have axes labeled? Curves named? Equilibrium points marked P* and Q*? Shifts shown with arrows? New equilibrium labeled differently?
🔗
Master the Policy Chain
The AP Macro exam loves asking you to trace a policy through 3–4 markets. Practice the chain: Fed action → money market → loanable funds → AD-AS → Phillips Curve → FOREX. Being able to "tell the story" step-by-step earns full points.
🧮
Know Your Multiplier Formulas Cold
Spending multiplier = 1/MPS. Tax multiplier = −MPC/MPS. Money multiplier = 1/RR. These appear in almost every exam. Given MPC = 0.8: MPS = 0.2, spending multiplier = 5, tax multiplier = −4. Practice until instant.
🏦
Monetary vs. Fiscal Policy — Know the Difference
Monetary = Federal Reserve, adjusts money supply and interest rates. Fiscal = Congress + President, adjusts spending and taxes. They're conducted by different institutions. On the FRQ, never say "the government raises interest rates" — that's the Fed's job.
🔄
Short Run vs. Long Run — Always Specify
The AP exam constantly tests your ability to distinguish short-run and long-run effects. In the short run: wages are sticky, SRAS can shift. In the long run: wages adjust, economy returns to potential GDP. Always state which run you're analyzing.
💱
Exchange Rate Direction Tricks
High interest rates → currency appreciates. High inflation → currency depreciates. Trade surplus → currency tends to appreciate. Appreciate = currency buys MORE foreign stuff. Depreciate = currency buys LESS. Draw the FOREX graph for any exchange rate question.
🚫
Never Shift Both Curves to Fix a Gap
To close a recessionary gap with fiscal policy: shift AD right. To show long-run self-correction: shift SRAS right. Don't shift both on the same graph unless explicitly showing two different scenarios. One policy = one shift.
📝
FRQ Vocabulary Matters
Say "aggregate demand increases" not "GDP goes up." Say "real interest rate rises" not "interest rates go up." Say "the price level rises" not "there is inflation." Graders award points for precise economic language — vague answers earn no credit even if conceptually right.
📉
Stagflation = Left SRAS Shift — Always
Stagflation (rising unemployment + rising inflation simultaneously) is caused by a leftward SRAS shift (negative supply shock). It cannot be caused by an AD shift alone. When you see "oil price spike" or "drought" → SRAS shifts left → stagflation → no easy policy fix.
🌍
Open Economy Linkage
A common FRQ: expansionary monetary policy → ↓ interest rates → ↓ demand for USD → USD depreciates → ↑ exports, ↓ imports → ↑ net exports → AD shifts further right. This open economy amplification is frequently tested in Part (e) of long FRQs.
🌙
Night Before Strategy
Review the 6 key graphs (PPC, AD-AS, Money Market, Loanable Funds, Phillips Curve, FOREX) and practice drawing them from scratch. Review the multiplier formulas. Get 8 hours of sleep — you've already done the work. Bring two pencils and a calculator.
